ENGin

a program that pairs English-speaking volunteers with Ukrainian students to help the latter improve and strengthen their English speaking skills

what i've learned

While the concept had seemed relatively bizarre to me, the ENGin program promptly presented itself as the enriching experience that it is. I had started the program talking to four Ukrainian teens, all of whom were around my age. Talking to random strangers to better engage their English-speaking abilities had seemed like an obvious advancement on their part, but I quickly began to realize the cultural growth on my end. As humans, we tend to become absorbed in our world only—our community, town, state, country—and forget how big the Earth actually is. There is an entire world beyond our surroundings, a world that most will never entirely see. ENGin, however, allowed me to broaden my perspectives and connect with people on the other side of the globe, giving me a new viewpoint and appreciation for life.

Brilliant Detroit

a virtual mentoring program that pairs volunteers with young students to encourage a passion for reading and to improve their literacy abilities

what i've learned

As someone who had always been an avid lover of books, it feels nothing short of miraculous to be spreading the joy of reading. Before the program presumed a short temporary break at the beginning of September, I had been mentoring Lyricc, an adorable first-grader, every Tuesday and Thursday. While the sessions were usually short, I enjoyed learning small tidbits about her life—the pink, wooden music box given as a present, the chocolate lab that could be seen lounging on the couch, and her younger brother popping in every so often to say hi. It's astonishing how these short moments every week could give me such an extraordinary glimpse into a total stranger's life and remind me of the joy I had when I was Lyricc's age, absorbed in a book and all of the adventures it contained.

Village Book Builders

an international program that builds libraries in developing countries and virtually connects students of the said countries to mentors

what i've learned

Growing up in a place of privilege, we often tend to forget exactly how fortunate we are. Among the various commodities we underappreciate is education, a resource so powerful and potent that for many, it is the golden ticket to a new life. The majority of the world, fortunately, seems to realize the importance of this wealth of knowledge with the right to education declared as a human one. However, while its significance is understood, the implication of its practice is unsatisfactory worldwide. Village Book Builders seeks to bridge this learning gap with the building of libraries in developing countries and communities and virtually connects students in those communities with mentors. I am mentoring two young girls in Belize, and the program has considerably expanded my horizons and reemphasized my appreciation for the education that I am lucky enough to have.
